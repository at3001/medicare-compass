Designing for our loved ones

Medicare Compass is an educational website designed to support immigrants on their Medicare journey. Below is a peek into how we brought the idea to life!

The problem

The idea for Medicare Compass came out of a desire to support our loved ones in an increasingly important aspect of their lives: managing their health into old age. As a team of immigrants and children of immigrants, we have seen firsthand the challenges of navigating a new country with few existing connections and an abundance of language and cultural barriers. Layer on the notoriously complex and expensive US healthcare system, a system that even native-born Americans have difficulty navigating, and the challenge feels insurmountable.


This problem shows itself in the statistics. Immigrants are more likely to be uninsured, have no usual source of care other than the emergency room, and to skip or postpone care in the last 12 months compared to naturalized citizens¹. The idea of tackling Medicare enrollment came to mind after Lilly saw her mom, a 64-year old Chinese immigrant, attend 40 hours of webinar classes, meet with three insurance agents, and call several of her friends to try to determine which Medicare plan she should choose. Though Lilly’s mom had the time and resources to piece together an informed decision, we knew many immigrants are not so fortunate and fall trap to lifetime penalties, denial of coverage, and unaffordable medical bills.

Our first step in our design process was to interview five other immigrants who had recently gone through the Medicare enrollment process. We noticed the following recurring pain points:

Lack of guidance

When asked how they went about starting to learn about Medicare, almost all interviewees described a feeling of “I didn’t even know where to begin.” Most interviewees were used to choosing from the 1-3 health insurance plans their employers offered, rather than comparing the dozens of different pathways you can take with Medicare coverage. Due to lack of connections in the US, interviewees had not seen older family members go through Medicare enrollment and had no idea what to expect or what questions to ask.



I felt scared and totally lost when starting the Medicare process.
– Mr. Liu, age 67 🇹🇼

I wish the health agent could have explained more to me… she didn’t give me much education and I also don’t know how to ask questions. I was just enrolled automatically and didn’t get the chance to really explore.
– Ms. Sun, age 68 🇨🇳

I thought [Medicare] would be simple and cover everything. Medicare was not that simple.
– Ms. Zhao, age 64 🇨🇳

Information overload

Most interviewees cited Google as one of their first information sources for Medicare, and recounted being overwhelmed by the number of search results that would appear. Though Google offered information on almost any question they asked, each question required a painstaking amount of time in order to piece together a cohesive answer. Interviewees desired a single source of truth that was capable of addressing their specific situation, without forcing them to sift through dozens of articles to find what they needed.



I really wanted to talk to a person [about Medicare] because there’s so much information, so many articles.
– Ms. Zhao, age 64 🇨🇳

There’s no single source - you need to go to a bunch of Youtube videos to answer each individual question, or go to an article and then need to click on an embedded article. You really need to spend time to research this.
– Mr. Xia, age 67 🇨🇳

Language and accessibility barriers

Even for interviewees with high English proficiency, reading about Medicare outside their native tongue is exhausting and time consuming. For the few resources that do exist in their native language, interviewees had difficulty applying their learnings later on as they had trouble recognizing the terms in English. Outside of language, some interviewees expressed frustration with the UI of Medicare articles and Google results in general, such as the font being too small for an older person to read.



If you don’t have experience with Medicare, it’s hard to match the Chinese term to the English term – and if you need to reference another website, it’s hard to tell if they are talking about the same thing you read before because multiple terms can refer to the same thing.
– Mr. Xia, age 67 🇨🇳

The font is way too small to read. I would rather have a video or lecture, reading is too hard.
– Ms. Zhao, age 64 🇨🇳

Ideation

Given this set of pain points, our team began brainstorming feature ideas that could address each one.

Lack of guidance

  • Guided course format: Many of our interviewees had no knowledge base of Medicare and no clear place to go to start building one – so we decided we would create that starting place. We wanted to emulate successful education apps such as Duolingo that guides the user through what they need to learn and when through a single path.
  • Personalized content: We envisioned our website to have dynamic content that changed based on information the user provides throughout the course. It’s simple enough for users to research generic questions such as “What is Medicare?” on their own, but things become complicated when they get into the specifics: “How do I enroll if I plan on retiring when I’m age 67 and won’t reach the 5-year anniversary of my green card status until age 68?”

Information overload

  • Bite-sized information: Reading a large wall of text is never easy, let alone text filled with unfamiliar healthcare terms and complicated edge cases. Looking towards Duolingo as an example again, we wanted to present our content in bite-sized pieces so the information would be more digestible.
  • Tooltip pop-outs: Given the amount of technical healthcare jargon in Medicare, we wanted a way for users to be able to read more about terms they may be confused by, without filling the page with too much unnecessary information. We decided to give users the option of whether they would like to see additional information through tooltip pop-outs that appear when users click highlighted terms.

Language and accessibility barriers

  • Dual language UI: Inspired by how The New York Times offers certain articles with the English and Chinese translations side by side, we thought we could use a similar design to allow users to read with ease in their native language while still being exposed to English Medicare terminology.

Iteration

After creating our initial set of feature ideas, our team went through three main prototype iterations over the course of the semester, collecting feedback from prospective users and classmates between each one. This design cycle process resulted in the following feature additions to our initial idea.

  • Saving notes: Early on in the course, the CIS5120 teaching staff pushed us to think beyond simply educating users and consider how we can tangibly help them with the next step of their Medicare journey. We re-examined the user journeys of our interviewees and noticed they often began with basic research on their own, before scheduling a meeting with an insurance agent to ask detailed questions. To support this process, we added a feature that allows users to save notes while they are going through the course and receive a PDF of their personalized questions and content to review with their insurance agent later on.
  • Comprehension quizzes: One of our later sources of feedback was a 70-year old UPenn professor, who recommended adding comprehension quizzes to our course for its pedagogical benefits. We noticed other education apps such as Duolingo also include small quizzes as a way to help users retain information, and we saw in testing that users seemed more motivated to read through the content carefully when they knew they would receive a quiz.
  • Paragraph-sized information bites: Throughout our rounds of prototypes, we received conflicting feedback on the ideal size of our bites of information. Originally we had our content appear in bites of 1-2 sentences, similar to how Duolingo displays its long-form reading comprehension exercises, but some users found this size tedious while others found it easily digestible. To put the question to rest, we ran a controlled user study with our classmates and ultimately decided on paragraph-sized bites with questionnaires and quizzes appearing all at once.
  • General UI cleanup: Along with the major feature changes above, our team was constantly re-evaluating our UI and looking for ways to make our website more minimalistic and modern. Some of the smaller changes we made include changing our color palette to be lighter, making our font all sans serif, and making sure our elements scaled based on viewport size.

User Reception

Our final working product was well received by users. Users particularly appreciated the dual language UI, which aided comprehension, alongside the bite-sized and digestible content which simplified the complexities associated with Medicare. Features including content-specific notes that are saved in a final printout, and the customized timeline were further stressed by interviewees as having potential in aiding immigrant seniors in their journeys. Overall, we were happy with the reception to MedicareCompass, which strengthened our conviction in this being a great first step in the journey of making Medicare easy and accessible for immigrant seniors.

Moving forward

We recognize what we have built so far is only the beginning – tackling inequity in Medicare accessibility among immigrants is a complex problem, but we hope we were able to raise awareness of the issue through this project. We would like to send a huge thanks to the immigrant voices who helped shape this project, as well as our CIS5120 teaching staff and classmates for their generous feedback and guidance. Medicare Compass is far from complete; there is still a long list of features and content that we would have loved to build out, but did not have the time for. However, we are always keeping open the possibility of picking up this project again in the future!
